hi, i’m makda.

I’m Makda, a junior studying Interaction Design and Economics at GWU. My work is shaped by the media I've consumed — games, music, and animated shows.Even this website design was inspired by AdHoc Studio's game Dispatch (more specifically the hacking minigame module).

Kanye once told a story about a two-year-old jumping on a coffee table. Someone yells, “Get down,” and the table goes from being something that made them feel like Superman to something you have to think about. Over time, you collect all these coffee tables, constantly calculating yourself into mental traffic.

Rules matter; foundations keep us from falling through the floor. But the goal isn't obedient conformity — it's remembering why we jumped in the first place. That way of thinking carries through my work itself — jumping between posters, video editing, code, and concert spaces, not as separate disciplines but an encouragement of exploration and complexity.

Tools: Photoshop • After Effects • Indesign + Illustrator • Figma

vool — figma prototype

VOOL is a mobile app concept for logging and revisiting concerts you’ve attended. To the left is a playthrough of the prototype using my own concert photos. The idea stems from my frustration with trying to find specific shows in a camera roll where everything visually blurs together. I employed a design sprint methodology to inform the final design, including a focus group review.

genre.exe — Video Playthrough

Contemporary streaming platforms have effectively taken over genre classification, determining how music is labeled, grouped, and understood at scale. These classifications are not just a means for organization; they shape listener perception, influence recommendation systems, and solidify how songs and artists are culturally framed over time.

genre.exe is a web-based genre exploration tool using iTunes, Last.Fm, and Wikipedia APIs. Searching a song returns community-generated tags from Last.fm rather than platform-assigned genres, shifting classification away from corporate systems and toward listener interpretation. Each tag links to its corresponding Wikipedia category, and if a tag doesn't have a wikipedia page, I included a note talking about how music creation is evolving past such rigid categorization.
